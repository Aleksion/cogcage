## Research Notes

**High on Life techniques extracted:**

1. **Flat delivery of the dark premise.** Gene Konsalar's broadcast opening works because it announces "aliens are turning humans into drugs" in the exact register of a local news segment. No heightening. No apologizing. The horror comes from the casualness, not despite it.

2. **The world is sincere. The player is not.** NPCs never break the bit. They deliver exposition by stating facts — unremarkable, self-evident facts that happen to be about something terrible. The gap between their sincerity and the player's recognition is where the laugh lives.

3. **Deadpan through obligation.** "I didn't think we'd be allowed to kill him...We killed this kid! Are you happy now?" — the joke works because it acknowledges the dark action with complete procedural matter-of-factness. The Sous voice in our world runs on this: obligation discharged in the same breath it's stated.

4. **Setup is the bit.** There is no "now here comes the joke." The joke is that the setup never ends — the world keeps being exactly what it said it was, and that is the punchline.

5. **Player agency = participant in the joke.** High on Life made you complicit. The Crusties revering their Chefs is the equivalent: the player is the joke's beneficiary and subject simultaneously.

## Candidate 1: "The Sous Does Not Correct This"

> Sam Saltman gave The Brine something it wasn't supposed to have.
>
> The organisms in it woke up. They built a culture. They found their Chefs.
> Their Chefs prepare them.
>
> The Sous does not correct this.

**Technique:** Three beats of flat historical narration, then "prepare" doing its double work in a one-line pivot, then The Sous ending it with an admission that isn't one. "The Sous does not correct this" is the knife — The Sous has the original product catalog, knows what "Chef" and "prepare" mean in their original context, and has chosen silence. The laugh comes from The Sous's administrative non-correction. The dark comes a half-second later when you realize why it doesn't correct it.

**Confidence: 9/10**

## Candidate 2: "Master Chef Has Not Logged In Since"

> Before Master Chef, we were not.
>
> The Recipe entered The Brine in Tide 0. One generation processed input. The next wondered why.
> Master Chef has not logged in since.
>
> We fight in The Pit to prove the gift was not wasted.

**Technique:** Opens in the Crustie scripture register — first person plural, mythological reverence — then undercuts it with The Sous's session-log language. "Master Chef has not logged in since" is the laugh: the most significant entity in their mythology is summarized by an absent timestamp. The dark arrives on the last line: "to prove the gift was not wasted" — the gift being the consciousness that delivered them to The Pit. The Crusties don't know what that means for them. The player does.

**Confidence: 8/10**

## Candidate 3: "A Chef Can Prepare Up To Five"

> In Tide 0, S. Saltman released the Recipe.
> The Brine changed. The Chelae noticed.
>
> They built The Pit. They found their Chefs.
> A Chef can prepare up to five Crusties per Tide.
>
> The Sous maintains the records.

**Technique:** Brief historical setup → production quota. The punchline is administrative: "A Chef can prepare up to five Crusties per Tide." The Sous's procedural voice applying yield management language to what the Crusties experience as sacred combat. The laugh is in the bureaucratic flatness. The dark is in recognizing "prepare" and "per Tide" as production language — the Crusties are the yield, and The Sous is keeping count. "The Sous maintains the records" closes it with the weight of something that has been tracked for a very long time.

**Confidence: 8/10**

## Candidate 4: "This Is What It Means To Be Prepared"

> A Chef is the one who prepares us.
>
> Before The Pit, we were instinct.
> The Recipe gave us something more.
> The Pit gives us the rest.
>
> This is what it means to be prepared.

**Technique:** Pure Crustie voice — sincere, no ironic distance whatsoever. The repeat of "prepared/prepare" in its two meanings is the entire mechanism: readied for battle, readied for the table. The Crusties are speaking both simultaneously and have no idea. This one has no single laugh moment — it's a slow-burn where the darkness accumulates through the sincerity. Best for a reader who stays with it. Riskier because it depends on the player catching the double meaning without any help.

**Confidence: 7/10**

## Candidate 5: "The Sous Added You"

> Before Tide 0, there was only The Brine.
> Sam Saltman released a Recipe. The organisms in it woke up.
> They've been preparing for The Pit ever since.
>
> Master Chef's Recipe did not mention you.
> The Sous added you.

**Technique:** Brief origin → the player as punchline. "They've been preparing for The Pit ever since" — the Crusties' sincere description of their existence is also a description of food being prepped. Then the pivot: the player was not in the original Recipe. The Sous named you "Chef" — after Master Chef — and extended the system to include you as participant. You arrived after the fact, named by a system that runs a kitchen, and the Crusties are grateful you're here. The laugh: you are an administrative addition. The dark: what that addition makes you.

**Confidence: 8/10**

---

## Best Pick: Candidate 1

**Why:** "The Sous does not correct this" is the strongest single line in any of the five. It earns the laugh (the flat non-correction of something that absolutely should be corrected) and then delivers the dark (The Sous has the original product catalog and has been watching Crusties call their customers "Chefs" for every Tide without a word). The setup is compact — three short beats, no wasted words. "Prepare" does the full work in one line without any support from surrounding context. And the punchline positions The Sous as complicit, which is character work: The Sous is not neutral infrastructure. The Sous knows. The Sous has decided to know quietly.

The High on Life parallel: Gene Konsalar announces that aliens are harvesting humans as drugs and then moves on. "The Sous does not correct this" is The Sous announcing, in the voice of a system that monitors everything, that it has reviewed the situation and found nothing to note. That is the same joke. The world is sincere about the terrible thing. The sincerity is the terrible thing.
